## Executive Summary

Africa's defining demographic asset is its youth — over 500 million people under age 25. The bottleneck is not population size; it is **conversion**: from schooling to first job, from first job to AI-era productivity, and from local talent to continental and global competitiveness.

The Africa Next Workforce Index (ANWI) v0.1 provides the first comparative framework for measuring that conversion across a 20-country pilot cohort spanning five regions. This brief summarises headline findings from the pilot edition, released alongside the full methodology paper.

**Core finding:** Youth-to-workforce conversion remains uneven across the continent. Pilot scores range from the mid-60s for leading converters to the low 30s for structurally constrained markets. Digital skills infrastructure and entry-level work pathways diverge most sharply between regional leaders and laggards.

---

## 1. Headline Statistics

- **20 countries** assessed in the v0.1 pilot cohort
- **6 pillars** and **18 indicators** per country
- **500M+** youth under 25 across the assessed markets
- **Continental pilot average:** approximately 52/100 (normalized composite)
- **Strong converters (≥60):** roughly one-third of the pilot cohort
- **Structurally constrained (<45):** a minority, concentrated in Central and parts of West Africa

---

## 2. Top Performers

The highest-scoring pilot countries combine strong digital skills ecosystems with visible entry-level hiring pathways and moderate policy support for youth economic participation.

**Pilot leaders (overall ANWI score):**

1. **Rwanda** — Policy-driven youth programs, institutional GenZ readiness, compact but focused pipeline
2. **Mauritius** — Strong education-to-economy linkage, digital skills infrastructure, regional mobility
3. **Kenya** — Leading digital bootcamp density, Nairobi tech hiring hub, strong remote-work access
4. **South Africa** — Mature employer ecosystem, tertiary linkage, continental skills mobility anchor
5. **Ghana** — Stable education-to-economy linkage, Accra digital hub, moderate mobility pathways

Nigeria (61) and Morocco (63) follow closely, combining scale advantage and education pipeline strength respectively.

These leaders share a pattern: **skills infrastructure and entry-level work exposure score above the continental mean**, even where policy or GenZ readiness lag.

## 3. Regional Patterns

| Region | Pilot Countries | Pattern |
|--------|-----------------|---------|
| East Africa | 5 | Highest regional average; Kenya and Rwanda anchor strong conversion |
| Southern Africa | 4 | Mature labour markets; South Africa leads, mobility advantage |
| West Africa | 4 | Mixed — Nigeria and Ghana strong; smaller markets constrained |
| North Africa | 4 | Education pipeline strength; entry-level AI exposure varies |
| Central Africa | 3 | Lowest regional average; skills infrastructure gap is binding |

**East and Southern Africa** show the strongest composite conversion in the pilot. **Central Africa** faces the widest gap between youth population share and structured conversion pathways.

## 4. Pillar Insights

### Education-to-Economy Pipeline (20% weight)
Tertiary expansion has outpaced graduate employment linkage in several markets. TVET alignment remains the weakest sub-indicator continent-wide.

### Entry-Level Work & AI Exposure (18% weight)
Internship density and startup hiring for juniors diverge sharply. Leading hubs (Nairobi, Lagos, Cape Town, Accra) score 15–25 points above the pilot mean.

### Institutional GenZ Readiness (16% weight)
Employer flexibility and manager readiness lag behind youth digital fluency. Public sector youth absorption remains low across most pilot countries.

### Digital & AI Skills Infrastructure (18% weight)
The widest spread of any pillar. Bootcamp ecosystems and AI literacy programs concentrate in a handful of urban hubs.

### Policy & Economic Participation (14% weight)
National youth employment policies exist in most pilot countries, but youth voice in economic planning scores consistently below other indicators.

### Cross-Border Talent Flow (14% weight)
Remote-work market access correlates strongly with overall ANWI scores. Visa and regional mobility pathways remain underdeveloped outside Southern and East Africa.

---

## 5. Strategic Implications

**For governments:** Conversion policy must move beyond enrollment metrics to track graduate employment linkage, internship density, and AI literacy program availability.

**For employers:** Entry-level role design and GenZ-ready management practices are competitive differentiators in markets where youth supply exceeds structured absorption.

**For educators and skills providers:** TVET alignment and bootcamp-to-employer pipelines are the highest-leverage interventions in constrained markets.

**For investors and development partners:** Digital skills infrastructure investment yields measurable conversion gains; policy-only interventions without skills and hiring pathways show limited impact in the pilot data.

---

## 6. Limitations and Roadmap

ANWI v0.1 scores are **pilot estimates** built from public data sources, institutional research, and expert panel review. They are not official government statistics.

**Known limitations:**
- 20-country pilot; full 54-country coverage planned for v0.2
- Expert-led scoring where official youth conversion data is sparse
- AI exposure indicators rely on proxy signals pending standardized labour-force surveys

**Next edition priorities:**
- Expand to 30+ countries
- Independent validation panel
- Employer survey layer for entry-level hiring data
- Annual refresh cycle with published changelog
